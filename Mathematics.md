## Chapter 1: Introduction to Mathematics

### 1.1 Importance and Applications of Mathematics

Mathematics is a fundamental discipline that has been a cornerstone of human knowledge for millennia. It is the language through which we understand the universe, from the patterns in nature to the principles governing technology and engineering. Mathematics is not just about numbers; it involves patterns, structures, and logical analysis. Its applications span various fields, including science, engineering, economics, medicine, and social sciences.

- **Science and Engineering**: Mathematics provides the tools for scientists and engineers to model natural phenomena, analyze data, and solve complex problems. For example, calculus is essential in physics for understanding motion and change, while statistics is crucial in biology for analyzing genetic variations.
- **Economics and Finance**: Mathematics is used in economics to model markets, optimize resources, and analyze economic trends. Financial mathematics helps in pricing options, managing risks, and making investment decisions.
- **Medicine and Biology**: Mathematical models help in understanding the spread of diseases, analyzing medical data, and improving diagnostic techniques. Biostatistics is critical in designing and interpreting clinical trials.
- **Social Sciences**: Mathematics aids in analyzing social data, modeling social behaviors, and making informed decisions in policy-making.

### 1.2 Mathematical Notation and Symbols

Mathematics has a unique language composed of symbols and notations that allow for concise and precise communication of ideas. Understanding these symbols is crucial for learning and applying mathematical concepts.

- **Basic Symbols**:
  - `+` (plus): Addition
  - `−` (minus): Subtraction
  - `×` (times) or `*` (asterisk): Multiplication
  - `÷` (division) or `/` (slash): Division
  - \`=` (equals): Equality
  - `<` (less than), `>` (greater than)
  - `≤` (less than or equal to), `≥` (greater than or equal to)

- **Advanced Symbols**:
  - `√` (square root): Example, √9 = 3
  - `∑` (sigma): Summation notation, used to sum a series of terms
  - `π` (pi): Ratio of the circumference of a circle to its diameter, approximately 3.14159
  - `∫` (integral): Integration symbol in calculus
  - `∞` (infinity): Represents an unbounded quantity

- **Set Notation**:
  - `{ }`: Curly braces denote a set. Example, {1, 2, 3}
  - `∈`: Element of. Example, 2 ∈ {1, 2, 3}
  - `∪`: Union of sets
  - `∩`: Intersection of sets

### 1.3 Problem-Solving Techniques

Problem-solving is at the heart of mathematics. Developing strong problem-solving skills is essential for success in mathematics and its applications. Here are some key techniques:

- **Understand the Problem**: Carefully read and analyze the problem statement. Identify what is given and what needs to be found.
- **Devise a Plan**: Consider various strategies to tackle the problem. Some common strategies include:
  - **Drawing a Diagram**: Visual representation can simplify complex problems.
  - **Looking for Patterns**: Identifying patterns can lead to generalizations and solutions.
  - **Breaking Down the Problem**: Divide the problem into smaller, more manageable parts.
  - **Using Algebraic Methods**: Set up equations that model the problem.
- **Execute the Plan**: Implement the chosen strategy step-by-step. Perform calculations carefully and check each step.
- **Review and Reflect**: Once a solution is found, review the process and the result. Verify the solution by plugging it back into the original problem. Reflect on the method used and consider alternative approaches.

## Chapter 2: Arithmetic

### 2.1 Basic Operations

Arithmetic is the branch of mathematics dealing with the properties and manipulation of numbers. The four basic operations are addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.

#### 2.1.1 Addition

Addition is the process of combining two or more quantities. The symbol for addition is `+`.

- **Example**: \(7 + 3 = 10\)

#### 2.1.2 Subtraction

Subtraction is the process of taking one quantity away from another. The symbol for subtraction is `-`.

- **Example**: \(10 - 3 = 7\)

#### 2.1.3 Multiplication

Multiplication is the process of combining equal groups. The symbol for multiplication is `×` or `*`.

- **Example**: \(4 × 3 = 12\)

#### 2.1.4 Division

Division is the process of distributing a quantity into equal parts. The symbol for division is `÷` or `/`.

- **Example**: \(12 ÷ 3 = 4\)

### 2.2 Properties of Numbers

Understanding the different types of numbers and their properties is fundamental in arithmetic.

#### 2.2.1 Integers

Integers are whole numbers that can be positive, negative, or zero.

- **Examples**: \(-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\)

#### 2.2.2 Fractions

Fractions represent parts of a whole. They consist of a numerator and a denominator.

- **Example**: \(\frac{3}{4}\)

#### 2.2.3 Decimals

Decimals are another way to represent fractions. They use a decimal point to separate the whole number part from the fractional part.

- **Example**: \(0.75\)

### 2.3 Factors, Multiples, and Prime Numbers

#### 2.3.1 Factors

Factors are numbers that divide another number exactly without leaving a remainder.

- **Example**: Factors of 12 are \(1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12\)

#### 2.3.2 Multiples

Multiples are the result of multiplying a number by an integer.

- **Example**: Multiples of 3 are \(3, 6, 9, 12, 15, \ldots\)

#### 2.3.3 Prime Numbers

Prime numbers are numbers greater than 1 that have no divisors other than 1 and themselves.

- **Examples**: \(2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, \ldots\)

### 2.4 Order of Operations

When performing arithmetic operations, it is important to follow the correct order of operations to obtain the right result. The order of operations can be remembered using the acronym PEMDAS:

- **P**: Parentheses
- **E**: Exponents
- **MD**: Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
- **AS**: Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)

- **Example**: \(3 + 6 × (5 + 4) ÷ 3 - 7\)
  1. Evaluate parentheses: \(3 + 6 × 9 ÷ 3 - 7\)
  2. Perform multiplication and division from left to right: \(3 + 54 ÷ 3 - 7\)
  3. Continue multiplication and division: \(3 + 18 - 7\)
  4. Perform addition and subtraction from left to right: \(21 - 7 = 14\)

### 2.5 Arithmetic with Fractions

#### 2.5.1 Adding and Subtracting Fractions

To add or subtract fractions, they must have a common denominator.

- **Example**: \(\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{2}{4} = \frac{3}{4}\)
- **Example**: \(\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\)

#### 2.5.2 Multiplying and Dividing Fractions

To multiply fractions, multiply the numerators and the denominators. To divide fractions, multiply by the reciprocal of the divisor.

- **Example**: \(\frac{2}{3} × \frac{3}{4} = \frac{2 × 3}{3 × 4} = \frac{6}{12} = \frac{1}{2}\)
- **Example**: \(\frac{2}{3} ÷ \frac{3}{4} = \frac{2}{3} × \frac{4}{3} = \frac{2 × 4}{3 × 3} = \frac{8}{9}\)

### 2.6 Arithmetic with Decimals

#### 2.6.1 Adding and Subtracting Decimals

Align the decimal points and perform the addition or subtraction as with whole numbers.

- **Example**: \(1.5 + 2.35 = 3.85\)
- **Example**: \(5.6 - 3.2 = 2.4\)

#### 2.6.2 Multiplying and Dividing Decimals

To multiply, ignore the decimal points initially, then place the decimal in the product. To divide, move the decimal point in the divisor to make it a whole number and do the same in the dividend.

- **Example**: \(3.6 × 2.5 = 36 × 25 = 900\) (then place the decimal: \(9.00\))
- **Example**: \(4.5 ÷ 1.5 = 45 ÷ 15 = 3\)

## Chapter 3: Algebra

### 3.1 Variables and Expressions

Algebra involves using symbols, often letters, to represent numbers in equations and expressions. This abstraction allows for solving problems involving unknown values.

#### 3.1.1 Variables

A variable is a symbol used to represent an unknown value. Common variables include \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\).

- **Example**: In the expression \(2x + 3\), \(x\) is a variable.

#### 3.1.2 Algebraic Expressions

An algebraic expression is a combination of numbers, variables, and operations.

- **Example**: \(3x + 2y - 5\) is an algebraic expression.

### 3.2 Linear Equations and Inequalities

#### 3.2.1 Solving Linear Equations

A linear equation is an equation of the form \(ax + b = c\). To solve for \(x\), isolate the variable.

- **Example**: Solve \(2x + 3 = 7\).
  - Subtract 3 from both sides: \(2x = 4\)
  - Divide both sides by 2: \(x = 2\)

#### 3.2.2 Solving Linear Inequalities

A linear inequality is similar to a linear equation but uses inequality symbols (\(<\), \(\leq\), \(>\), \(\geq\)) instead of an equals sign.

- **Example**: Solve \(2x + 3 > 7\).
  - Subtract 3 from both sides: \(2x > 4\)
  - Divide both sides by 2: \(x > 2\)

### 3.3 Polynomials and Factoring

#### 3.3.1 Polynomials

A polynomial is an expression consisting of variables and coefficients, involving only the operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents.

- **Example**: \(3x^2 + 2x - 5\) is a polynomial.

#### 3.3.2 Factoring Polynomials

Factoring involves writing a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials.

- **Example**: Factor \(x^2 - 5x + 6\).
  - Find factors of 6 that add to -5: \(-2\) and \(-3\)
  - Write the factors: \((x - 2)(x - 3)\)

### 3.4 Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation is a polynomial equation of the second degree, typically in the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\).

#### 3.4.1 Solving Quadratic Equations by Factoring

- **Example**: Solve \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\).
  - Factor the quadratic: \((x - 2)(x - 3) = 0\)
  - Set each factor to zero: \(x - 2 = 0\) or \(x - 3 = 0\)
  - Solve for \(x\): \(x = 2\) or \(x = 3\)

#### 3.4.2 Solving Quadratic Equations by the Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula is used when factoring is difficult. It is given by:
\[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\]

- **Example**: Solve \(2x^2 - 4x - 6 = 0\).
  - Identify \(a = 2\), \(b = -4\), and \(c = -6\)
  - Substitute into the formula:
	\[x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4 \cdot 2 \cdot (-6)}}{2 \cdot 2}\]
	\[x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 + 48}}{4}\]
	\[x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{64}}{4}\]
	\[x = \frac{4 \pm 8}{4}\]
	\[x = 3\] or \[x = -1\]

## Chapter 4: Geometry

### 4.1 Points, Lines, and Angles

Geometry is the branch of mathematics concerned with the properties and relations of points, lines, surfaces, and solids.

#### 4.1.1 Points and Lines

- **Point**: A location in space with no dimensions, represented by a dot. (e.g., point \(A\)).
- **Line**: A straight one-dimensional figure extending infinitely in both directions. (e.g., line \(AB\)).
- **Line Segment**: A part of a line with two endpoints. (e.g., segment \(AB\)).
- **Ray**: A part of a line that starts at one point and extends infinitely in one direction. (e.g., ray \(AB\)).

#### 4.1.2 Angles

An angle is formed by two rays with a common endpoint, called the vertex.

- **Types of Angles**:
  - **Acute Angle**: Less than 90 degrees.
  - **Right Angle**: Exactly 90 degrees.
  - **Obtuse Angle**: Greater than 90 degrees but less than 180 degrees.
  - **Straight Angle**: Exactly 180 degrees.

### 4.2 Triangles

Triangles are polygons with three sides and three angles. They are classified based on their side lengths and angles.

#### 4.2.1 Types of Triangles by Sides

- **Equilateral Triangle**: All three sides are equal.
- **Isosceles Triangle**: Two sides are equal.
- **Scalene Triangle**: All three sides are different lengths.

#### 4.2.2 Types of Triangles by Angles

- **Acute Triangle**: All angles are less than 90 degrees.
- **Right Triangle**: One angle is exactly 90 degrees.
- **Obtuse Triangle**: One angle is greater than 90 degrees.

#### 4.2.3 Pythagorean Theorem

In a right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

\[ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \]

- **Example**: In a right triangle with legs \(a = 3\) and \(b = 4\), the hypotenuse \(c\) is:
  \[ 3^2 + 4^2 = c^2 \]
  \[ 9 + 16 = c^2 \]
  \[ 25 = c^2 \]
  \[ c = 5 \]

### 4.3 Quadrilaterals

Quadrilaterals are polygons with four sides and four angles.

#### 4.3.1 Types of Quadrilaterals

- **Square**: Four equal sides and four right angles.
- **Rectangle**: Opposite sides are equal and four right angles.
- **Parallelogram**: Opposite sides are parallel and equal.
- **Rhombus**: Four equal sides with opposite angles equal.
- **Trapezoid**: Only one pair of parallel sides.

### 4.4 Circles

A circle is a set of points in a plane that are all the same distance from a given point, called the center.

#### 4.4.1 Parts of a Circle

- **Radius**: A segment from the center to any point on the circle.
- **Diameter**: A segment that passes through the center of the circle and has endpoints on the circle. It is twice the radius.
- **Circumference**: The distance around the circle.
  - **Formula**: \( C = 2\pi r \) where \(r\) is the radius.
- **Area**: The space inside the circle.
  - **Formula**: \( A = \pi r^2 \)

### 4.5 Perimeter, Area, and Volume

#### 4.5.1 Perimeter

The perimeter is the distance around a two-dimensional shape.

- **Example**: Perimeter of a rectangle:
  \[ P = 2(l + w) \]
  where \(l\) is the length and \(w\) is the width.

#### 4.5.2 Area

The area is the measure of the space inside a two-dimensional shape.

- **Example**: Area of a rectangle:
  \[ A = lw \]
  where \(l\) is the length and \(w\) is the width.

#### 4.5.3 Volume

The volume is the measure of the space inside a three-dimensional object.

- **Example**: Volume of a rectangular prism:
  \[ V = lwh \]
  where \(l\) is the length, \(w\) is the width, and \(h\) is the height.

## Chapter 5: Trigonometry

### 5.1 Trigonometric Ratios and Functions

Trigonometry is the branch of mathematics that deals with the relationships between the sides and angles of triangles, particularly right triangles. The basic trigonometric functions are sine (sin), cosine (cos), and tangent (tan).

#### 5.1.1 Right Triangle Definitions

In a right triangle, the hypotenuse is the longest side, and the other two sides are the adjacent and opposite sides relative to a given angle.

- **Sine (sin)**: The ratio of the length of the opposite side to the hypotenuse.
  \[ \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \]

- **Cosine (cos)**: The ratio of the length of the adjacent side to the hypotenuse.
  \[ \cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \]

- **Tangent (tan)**: The ratio of the length of the opposite side to the adjacent side.
  \[ \tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} \]

### 5.2 Right Triangle Trigonometry

Using the definitions of the trigonometric functions, we can solve problems involving right triangles.

#### 5.2.1 Finding Side Lengths

- **Example**: In a right triangle with \(\theta = 30^\circ\) and hypotenuse \(h = 10\):
  \[ \sin(30^\circ) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{10} \]
  \[ \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\text{opposite}}{10} \]
  \[ \text{opposite} = 5 \]

#### 5.2.2 Finding Angles

- **Example**: In a right triangle with opposite side \(5\) and adjacent side \(5\):
  \[ \tan(\theta) = \frac{5}{5} = 1 \]
  \[ \theta = \tan^{-1}(1) = 45^\circ \]

### 5.3 Graphs of Trigonometric Functions

The trigonometric functions can be graphed to show their behavior over a range of angles.

#### 5.3.1 Sine and Cosine Functions

- **Sine Function**:
  \[ y = \sin(x) \]
  - Period: \(2\pi\)
  - Amplitude: 1

- **Cosine Function**:
  \[ y = \cos(x) \]
  - Period: \(2\pi\)
  - Amplitude: 1

#### 5.3.2 Tangent Function

- **Tangent Function**:
  \[ y = \tan(x) \]
  - Period: \(\pi\)
  - Undefined at \(x = \frac{\pi}{2} + k\pi\), where \(k\) is an integer

### 5.4 Trigonometric Identities and Equations

Trigonometric identities are equations that are true for all values of the variable. They are useful for simplifying expressions and solving equations.

#### 5.4.1 Fundamental Identities

- **Pythagorean Identity**:
  \[ \sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x) = 1 \]

- **Reciprocal Identities**:
  \[ \csc(x) = \frac{1}{\sin(x)} \]
  \[ \sec(x) = \frac{1}{\cos(x)} \]
  \[ \cot(x) = \frac{1}{\tan(x)} \]

- **Quotient Identities**:
  \[ \tan(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)} \]
  \[ \cot(x) = \frac{\cos(x)}{\sin(x)} \]

#### 5.4.2 Solving Trigonometric Equations

- **Example**: Solve \(\sin(x) = \frac{1}{2}\) for \(0 \leq x < 2\pi\).
  \[ x = \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{5\pi}{6} \]

## Chapter 6: Calculus

### 6.1 Limits and Continuity

Calculus is the branch of mathematics that studies continuous change. It is divided into two main branches: differential calculus and integral calculus. Understanding limits is foundational to both.

#### 6.1.1 Limits

A limit describes the value that a function approaches as the input approaches a certain point.

- **Notation**: \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L\), where \(L\) is the limit of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches \(c\).

- **Example**: Find \(\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1)\).
  \[ \lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1) = 3(2) + 1 = 7 \]

#### 6.1.2 Continuity

A function is continuous if there are no breaks, holes, or jumps in its graph.

- **Definition**: A function \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = c\) if:
  1. \(f(c)\) is defined.
  2. \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\) exists.
  3. \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)\).

- **Example**: Determine if \(f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}\) is continuous at \(x = 1\).
  \[ f(x) = \frac{(x - 1)(x + 1)}{x - 1} = x + 1 \text{ for } x \neq 1 \]
  - As \(x \to 1\), \(f(x) = 2\), but \(f(1)\) is not defined, so it is not continuous at \(x = 1\).

### 6.2 Differentiation

Differentiation is the process of finding the derivative of a function, which measures the rate at which a function's value changes.

#### 6.2.1 Derivative

The derivative of a function \(f(x)\) at a point \(x = a\) is given by:
\[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} \]

- **Notation**: \(f'(x)\), \(\frac{df}{dx}\), or \(D_x f(x)\).

- **Example**: Find the derivative of \(f(x) = x^2\).
  \[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x + h)^2 - x^2}{h} \]
  \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h} \]
  \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} \]
  \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) = 2x \]

#### 6.2.2 Rules of Differentiation

- **Power Rule**: \(\frac{d}{dx} x^n = nx^{n-1}\)
- **Sum Rule**: \(\frac{d}{dx} [f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x)\)
- **Product Rule**: \(\frac{d}{dx} [f(x)g(x)] = f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x)\)
- **Quotient Rule**: \(\frac{d}{dx} \left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{g(x)^2}\)
- **Chain Rule**: \(\frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x))g'(x)\)

### 6.3 Applications of Differentiation

Differentiation has many applications, including finding slopes of tangents, determining rates of change, and solving problems in physics and engineering.

#### 6.3.1 Finding Tangents

The slope of the tangent line to the curve \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = a\) is \(f'(a)\).

- **Example**: Find the equation of the tangent line to \(y = x^2\) at \(x = 1\).
  \[ f'(x) = 2x \]
  \[ \text{Slope at } x = 1 \text{ is } 2 \]
  \[ \text{Point on the curve: } (1, 1^2) = (1, 1) \]
  \[ \text{Equation: } y - 1 = 2(x - 1) \]
  \[ y = 2x - 1 \]

#### 6.3.2 Optimization

Optimization involves finding the maximum or minimum values of a function.

- **Example**: Find the maximum value of \(f(x) = -x^2 + 4x + 1\).
  \[ f'(x) = -2x + 4 \]
  \[ \text{Set } f'(x) = 0 \text{ to find critical points: } -2x + 4 = 0 \]
  \[ x = 2 \]
  \[ \text{Evaluate } f(x) \text{ at } x = 2: f(2) = -2^2 + 4(2) + 1 = 5 \]
  \[ \text{Maximum value is } 5 \]

### 6.4 Integration

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. It involves finding the integral of a function, which represents the area under a curve.

#### 6.4.1 Indefinite Integrals

The indefinite integral of \(f(x)\) is the family of all antiderivatives of \(f(x)\), denoted by:
\[ \int f(x) \, dx \]

- **Example**: Find \(\int 2x \, dx\).
  \[ \int 2x \, dx = x^2 + C \]
  where \(C\) is the constant of integration.

#### 6.4.2 Definite Integrals

The definite integral of \(f(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is the net area under the curve \(y = f(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\), denoted by:
\[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx \]

- **Example**: Find \(\int_0^2 2x \, dx\).
  \[ \int_0^2 2x \, dx = \left[ x^2 \right]_0^2 = 2^2 - 0^2 = 4 \]

#### 6.4.3 Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus links differentiation and integration.

- **Part 1**: If \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) on \([a, b]\), then:
  \[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) \]

- **Part 2**: If \(F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt\), then:
  \[ F'(x) = f(x) \]

### 6.5 Applications of Integration

Integration is used in various applications, including finding areas, volumes, and solving differential equations.

#### 6.5.1 Area Under a Curve

- **Example**: Find the area under \(y = x^2\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 2\).
  \[ \int_0^2 x^2 \, dx = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_0^2 = \frac{2^3}{3} - \frac{0^3}{3} = \frac{8}{3} \]

#### 6.5.2 Volume of a Solid of Revolution

- **Example**: Find the volume of the solid formed by revolving \(y = x^2\) about the x-axis from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1\).
  \[ V = \pi \int_0^1 (x^2)^2 \, dx = \pi \int_0^1 x^4 \, dx = \pi \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} \right]_0^1 = \frac{\pi}{5} \]

## Chapter 7: Probability and Statistics

### 7.1 Basic Probability Concepts

Probability is the branch of mathematics that deals with the likelihood of events occurring. It is foundational for understanding and working with data in statistics.

#### 7.1.1 Definitions and Notation

- **Experiment**: A process that leads to one of several possible outcomes.
- **Sample Space (\(S\))**: The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
- **Event (\(E\))**: A subset of the sample space.
- **Probability of an Event (\(P(E)\))**: A measure of the likelihood that an event will occur, ranging from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

\[ P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total number of outcomes}} \]

#### 7.1.2 Types of Events

- **Independent Events**: Two events are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the occurrence of the other.
- **Dependent Events**: Two events are dependent if the occurrence of one affects the occurrence of the other.
- **Mutually Exclusive Events**: Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot occur at the same time.

### 7.2 Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics involve summarizing and organizing data so that it can be easily understood.

#### 7.2.1 Measures of Central Tendency

- **Mean (\(\bar{x}\))**: The average of a set of numbers.
  \[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n} \]

- **Median**: The middle value when a data set is ordered from least to greatest.
  - If the number of observations is odd, the median is the middle value.
  - If the number of observations is even, the median is the average of the two middle values.

- **Mode**: The value that appears most frequently in a data set.

#### 7.2.2 Measures of Dispersion

- **Range**: The difference between the highest and lowest values in a data set.
  \[ \text{Range} = \text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum} \]

- **Variance (\(\sigma^2\))**: The average of the squared differences from the mean.
  \[ \sigma^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n} \]

- **Standard Deviation (\(\sigma\))**: The square root of the variance.
  \[ \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} \]

### 7.3 Inferential Statistics

Inferential statistics involve making predictions or inferences about a population based on a sample of data.

#### 7.3.1 Probability Distributions

- **Discrete Probability Distributions**: Lists all possible outcomes of a random variable and their corresponding probabilities.
- **Continuous Probability Distributions**: Represented by a probability density function (pdf), which describes the likelihood of a random variable falling within a particular range of values.

#### 7.3.2 Normal Distribution

A continuous probability distribution that is symmetrical around the mean, describing a bell-shaped curve.

- **Properties**:
  - The mean, median, and mode are all equal.
  - It is defined by its mean (\(\mu\)) and standard deviation (\(\sigma\)).
  - Approximately 68% of the data falls within one standard deviation of the mean, 95% within two standard deviations, and 99.7% within three standard deviations (68-95-99.7 rule).

#### 7.3.3 Hypothesis Testing

A method of making decisions using data, whether from a controlled experiment or an observational study.

- **Steps in Hypothesis Testing**:
  1. **State the null hypothesis (\(H_0\))** and the alternative hypothesis (\(H_a\)).
  2. **Choose a significance level (\(\alpha\))**: Common values are 0.05 or 0.01.
  3. **Calculate the test statistic**: Depending on the type of data and hypothesis, different test statistics are used (e.g., t-test, z-test).
  4. **Determine the p-value**: The probability of observing the test results under the null hypothesis.
  5. **Make a decision**: If the p-value is less than \(\alpha\), reject the null hypothesis.

### 7.4 Data Collection and Analysis

Effective data collection and analysis are essential for accurate statistical inference.

#### 7.4.1 Data Collection Methods

- **Surveys**: Collecting data from a sample of individuals through questionnaires or interviews.
- **Experiments**: Conducting controlled tests to determine the effect of one variable on another.
- **Observational Studies**: Observing subjects in their natural environment without interference.

#### 7.4.2 Data Analysis Techniques

- **Descriptive Analysis**: Summarizing data using measures of central tendency and dispersion.
- **Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)**: Using graphical techniques to identify patterns, anomalies, and relationships in data.
- **Inferential Analysis**: Using sample data to make inferences about a larger population.

## Chapter 8: Discrete Mathematics

### 8.1 Logic and Set Theory

Discrete mathematics deals with objects that can assume only distinct, separated values. It is essential in computer science, cryptography, and combinatorial analysis.

#### 8.1.1 Logic

Logic is the study of reasoning. It provides a formal system for analyzing arguments and propositions.

- **Propositions**: Statements that are either true or false.
  - Example: "The sky is blue" (True), "2 + 2 = 5" (False).

- **Logical Operators**:
  - **AND (\(\land\))**: True if both propositions are true.
  - **OR (\(\lor\))**: True if at least one proposition is true.
  - **NOT (\(\neg\))**: Inverts the truth value.

- **Truth Tables**: A tool for evaluating the truth of propositions based on their logical operators.
  - Example: \(P \land Q\)
	\[
	\begin{array}{c|c|c}
	P & Q & P \land Q \\
	\hline
	T & T & T \\
	T & F & F \\
	F & T & F \\
	F & F & F \\
	\end{array}
	\]

#### 8.1.2 Set Theory

Set theory deals with the collection of objects.

- **Sets**: A collection of distinct objects.
  - Example: \(A = \{1, 2, 3\}\), \(B = \{2, 3, 4\}\).

- **Operations on Sets**:
  - **Union (\(\cup\))**: The set of elements in either set.
	\[ A \cup B = \{1, 2, 3, 4\} \]
  - **Intersection (\(\cap\))**: The set of elements in both sets.
	\[ A \cap B = \{2, 3\} \]
  - **Difference (\(\setminus\))**: The set of elements in one set but not the other.
	\[ A \setminus B = \{1\} \]
  - **Complement (\(\overline{A}\))**: The set of elements not in the set.

### 8.2 Combinatorics and Permutations

Combinatorics is the study of counting, arranging, and combining objects.

#### 8.2.1 Counting Principles

- **Addition Principle**: If there are \(m\) ways to do one thing and \(n\) ways to do another, and the two things cannot be done simultaneously, there are \(m + n\) ways to do either.
- **Multiplication Principle**: If there are \(m\) ways to do one thing and \(n\) ways to do another, there are \(m \times n\) ways to do both.

#### 8.2.2 Permutations

A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a specific order.

- **Formula**: The number of permutations of \(n\) objects is \(n!\) (n factorial).
  - Example: The number of ways to arrange 3 books is \(3! = 6\).

- **Permutations of \(n\) objects taken \(r\) at a time**:
  \[ P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n - r)!} \]

#### 8.2.3 Combinations

A combination is a selection of objects without regard to the order.

- **Formula**: The number of combinations of \(n\) objects taken \(r\) at a time is given by:
  \[ C(n, r) = \frac{n!}{r!(n - r)!} \]

### 8.3 Graph Theory and Networks

Graph theory is the study of graphs, which are mathematical structures used to model pairwise relations between objects.

#### 8.3.1 Graphs

- **Vertices (or Nodes)**: The points in a graph.
- **Edges**: The connections between vertices.

- **Types of Graphs**:
  - **Undirected Graph**: Edges have no direction.
  - **Directed Graph (Digraph)**: Edges have a direction.
  - **Weighted Graph**: Edges have weights or costs associated with them.

#### 8.3.2 Paths and Cycles

- **Path**: A sequence of edges that connects two vertices.
- **Cycle**: A path that starts and ends at the same vertex without repeating any edges or vertices.

#### 8.3.3 Trees

A tree is a special type of graph with no cycles, and it is connected.

- **Properties**:
  - There is exactly one path between any two vertices.
  - A tree with \(n\) vertices has \(n-1\) edges.

### 8.4 Algorithms and Complexity

Algorithms are step-by-step procedures for solving problems, and complexity theory deals with the efficiency of these algorithms.

#### 8.4.1 Algorithms

- **Definition**: A finite set of instructions that, if followed, accomplishes a particular task.
- **Examples**: Sorting algorithms (bubble sort, quicksort), search algorithms (binary search).

#### 8.4.2 Complexity

- **Time Complexity**: The amount of time an algorithm takes to complete as a function of the input size.
  - **Big O Notation (\(O\))**: A way to describe the upper bound of an algorithm's running time.
	- Example: \(O(n^2)\), \(O(n \log n)\).

- **Space Complexity**: The amount of memory an algorithm uses as a function of the input size.

## Chapter 9: Linear Algebra

### 9.1 Vectors and Matrices

Linear algebra is the branch of mathematics concerning linear equations, linear functions, and their representations through matrices and vector spaces.

#### 9.1.1 Vectors

A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Vectors are often represented as ordered lists of numbers.

- **Notation**: A vector in \(n\)-dimensional space is written as \(\mathbf{v} = (v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n)\).

- **Operations**:
  - **Addition**: \(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = (u_1 + v_1, u_2 + v_2, \ldots, u_n + v_n)\)
  - **Scalar Multiplication**: \(c\mathbf{v} = (cv_1, cv_2, \ldots, cv_n)\)
  - **Dot Product**: \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + \ldots + u_nv_n\)

#### 9.1.2 Matrices

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns.

- **Notation**: A matrix \(A\) with \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns is written as:
  \[
  A = \begin{pmatrix}
  a_{11} & a_{12} & \ldots & a_{1n} \\
  a_{21} & a_{22} & \ldots & a_{2n} \\
  \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
  a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \ldots & a_{mn}
  \end{pmatrix}
  \]

- **Operations**:
  - **Addition**: \(A + B = (a_{ij} + b_{ij})\)
  - **Scalar Multiplication**: \(cA = (ca_{ij})\)
  - **Matrix Multiplication**: The element in row \(i\), column \(j\) of \(AB\) is the dot product of the \(i\)th row of \(A\) and the \(j\)th column of \(B\).

### 9.2 Systems of Linear Equations

A system of linear equations is a collection of one or more linear equations involving the same set of variables.

#### 9.2.1 Solving Systems by Matrix Methods

- **Matrix Form**: A system of equations can be written as \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\), where \(A\) is the coefficient matrix, \(\mathbf{x}\) is the vector of variables, and \(\mathbf{b}\) is the vector of constants.

- **Gaussian Elimination**: A method for solving a system of linear equations. It involves using row operations to transform the matrix into row-echelon form.

  - **Row Operations**:
	1. Swap the positions of two rows.
	2. Multiply a row by a nonzero scalar.
	3. Add or subtract the scalar multiple of one row to another row.

  - **Example**: Solve the system:
	\[
	\begin{cases}
	x + y + z = 6 \\
	2y + 5z = -4 \\
	2x + 5y - z = 27
	\end{cases}
	\]

	Write the augmented matrix:
	\[
	\begin{pmatrix}
	1 & 1 & 1 & | & 6 \\
	0 & 2 & 5 & | & -4 \\
	2 & 5 & -1 & | & 27
	\end{pmatrix}
	\]

	Use row operations to solve.

### 9.3 Determinants and Eigenvalues

#### 9.3.1 Determinants

The determinant is a scalar value that can be computed from the elements of a square matrix and encodes certain properties of the matrix.

- **Notation**: The determinant of matrix \(A\) is denoted \(\det(A)\) or \(|A|\).

- **Calculation**: For a \(2 \times 2\) matrix:
  \[
  A = \begin{pmatrix}
  a & b \\
  c & d
  \end{pmatrix}, \quad \det(A) = ad - bc
  \]

  For larger matrices, determinants can be calculated using cofactor expansion.

#### 9.3.2 Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

An eigenvalue of a matrix \(A\) is a scalar \(\lambda\) such that there exists a nonzero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) (the eigenvector) satisfying \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}\).

- **Finding Eigenvalues**:
  - Solve the characteristic equation \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\), where \(I\) is the identity matrix.

- **Finding Eigenvectors**:
  - Substitute each eigenvalue \(\lambda\) into the equation \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}\) and solve for the vector \(\mathbf{v}\).

### 9.4 Applications of Linear Algebra

#### 9.4.1 Computer Graphics

Linear algebra is widely used in computer graphics to perform transformations such as rotation, scaling, and translation.

- **Example**: Rotation matrix in 2D:
  \[
  R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix}
  \cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\
  \sin \theta & \cos \theta
  \end{pmatrix}
  \]

#### 9.4.2 Systems of Differential Equations

Linear algebra techniques are used to solve systems of linear differential equations.

#### 9.4.3 Markov Chains

Markov chains use matrices to describe systems that transition from one state to another, where the probability of each transition depends only on the current state.

- **Transition Matrix**: Describes the probabilities of moving from one state to another.

## Chapter 10: Advanced Topics

### 10.1 Differential Equations

A differential equation is an equation that involves an unknown function and its derivatives. Differential equations are used to describe various phenomena in engineering, physics, economics, and other sciences.

#### 10.1.1 First-Order Differential Equations

A first-order differential equation involves the first derivative of the unknown function.

- **Example**: \(\frac{dy}{dx} + y = 0\)

- **Solution Method**: Separation of Variables
  - Separate the variables \(y\) and \(x\):
	\[ \frac{dy}{y} = -dx \]
  - Integrate both sides:
	\[ \int \frac{1}{y} dy = \int -1 dx \]
	\[ \ln|y| = -x + C \]
  - Solve for \(y\):
	\[ y = Ce^{-x} \]

#### 10.1.2 Second-Order Differential Equations

A second-order differential equation involves the second derivative of the unknown function.

- **Example**: \(y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0\)

- **Solution Method**: Characteristic Equation
  - Assume a solution of the form \(y = e^{rt}\).
  - Substitute into the differential equation to get the characteristic equation:
	\[ r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0 \]
  - Solve the characteristic equation:
	\[ (r - 1)(r - 2) = 0 \]
	\[ r = 1, r = 2 \]
  - General solution:
	\[ y(t) = C_1 e^{t} + C_2 e^{2t} \]

### 10.2 Complex Numbers

Complex numbers extend the concept of one-dimensional numbers to the two-dimensional complex plane by introducing the imaginary unit \(i\), where \(i^2 = -1\).

#### 10.2.1 Basic Operations

- **Addition and Subtraction**:
  \[ (a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)i \]
  \[ (a + bi) - (c + di) = (a - c) + (b - d)i \]

- **Multiplication**:
  \[ (a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i \]

- **Division**:
  \[ \frac{a + bi}{c + di} = \frac{(a + bi)(c - di)}{(c + di)(c - di)} = \frac{(ac + bd) + (bc - ad)i}{c^2 + d^2} \]

#### 10.2.2 Polar Form

- **Polar Form of a Complex Number**: \(z = r(\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)\)
  - Where \(r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\) is the magnitude and \(\theta = \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{b}{a}\right)\) is the argument.

- **Euler's Formula**:
  \[ e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i \sin \theta \]
  - Thus, \(z = re^{i\theta}\)

### 10.3 Fourier Analysis

Fourier analysis is a method of expressing a function as a sum of periodic components and recovering the signal from those components.

#### 10.3.1 Fourier Series

A Fourier series is a way to represent a function as the sum of simple sine and cosine waves.

- **Formula**:
  \[ f(x) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( a_n \cos \frac{2n\pi x}{L} + b_n \sin \frac{2n\pi x}{L} \right) \]
  - Where the coefficients \(a_0\), \(a_n\), and \(b_n\) are calculated as:
	\[ a_0 = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \, dx \]
	\[ a_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \cos \frac{2n\pi x}{L} \, dx \]
	\[ b_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \sin \frac{2n\pi x}{L} \, dx \]

#### 10.3.2 Fourier Transform

The Fourier transform generalizes Fourier series to non-periodic functions.

- **Formula**:
  \[ \hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) e^{-2\pi i \xi x} \, dx \]
  - The inverse Fourier transform is given by:
	\[ f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi \]

### 10.4 Numerical Methods

Numerical methods are techniques to approximate solutions for mathematical problems that cannot be solved analytically.

#### 10.4.1 Numerical Integration

- **Trapezoidal Rule**:
  \[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx \approx \frac{b - a}{2} \left( f(a) + f(b) \right) \]

- **Simpson's Rule**:
  \[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx \approx \frac{b - a}{6} \left( f(a) + 4f\left( \frac{a+b}{2} \right) + f(b) \right) \]

#### 10.4.2 Solving Nonlinear Equations

- **Newton's Method**:
  - Used to find successively better approximations to the roots (or zeroes) of a real-valued function.
  - Formula: \( x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} \)

## Chapter 11: Mathematical Modeling

Mathematical modeling is the process of using mathematics to represent, analyze, and solve real-world problems. This chapter will explore how to create, analyze, and interpret mathematical models.

### 11.1 Creating Mathematical Models

The process of creating a mathematical model involves several steps:

#### 11.1.1 Problem Definition

Clearly define the problem you want to solve. Identify the objectives, constraints, and key variables.

- **Example**: Modeling the spread of a disease in a population.

#### 11.1.2 Assumptions

Make assumptions to simplify the problem while retaining essential features. Assumptions help to formulate the model but should be reasonable and justifiable.

- **Example**: Assume a constant population size and that everyone is equally likely to contract the disease.

#### 11.1.3 Formulation

Translate the problem and assumptions into mathematical language. This step involves defining variables, parameters, and equations.

- **Example**: Use differential equations to describe the rate of infection and recovery.

### 11.2 Analyzing Mathematical Models

Once a model is formulated, analyze it to gain insights and make predictions.

#### 11.2.1 Solving Equations

Solve the equations analytically or numerically to find the behavior of the system over time.

- **Example**: Solve the differential equations describing disease spread to find the number of infected individuals over time.

#### 11.2.2 Sensitivity Analysis

Study how changes in parameters affect the model's outcomes. This helps to understand which variables are most influential.

- **Example**: Analyze how changes in the infection rate affect the peak number of infected individuals.

#### 11.2.3 Validation

Compare the model's predictions with real-world data to check its accuracy. Adjust the model if necessary.

- **Example**: Compare the predicted infection curve with actual infection data from a similar disease outbreak.

### 11.3 Interpreting Mathematical Models

Interpret the results of the model to make decisions or recommendations.

#### 11.3.1 Understanding Results

Translate the mathematical results back into the context of the original problem. Identify key insights and trends.

- **Example**: Determine when the peak infection will occur and how long the outbreak will last.

#### 11.3.2 Making Predictions

Use the model to make predictions about future behavior of the system.

- **Example**: Predict the total number of infected individuals and the impact of different intervention strategies.

#### 11.3.3 Communicating Findings

Effectively communicate the model's findings to stakeholders, including non-experts. Use visual aids like graphs and charts to illustrate key points.

- **Example**: Present a report to public health officials with recommendations for controlling the outbreak.

### 11.4 Applications in Science, Engineering, and Economics

Mathematical modeling is used in various fields to solve complex problems.

#### 11.4.1 Epidemiology

Models of disease spread help public health officials plan interventions and allocate resources.

- **Example**: The SIR (Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered) model predicts the spread of infectious diseases.

#### 11.4.2 Environmental Science

Models of ecosystems and climate change help predict the impact of human activities on the environment.

- **Example**: Models of carbon emissions and their impact on global warming.

#### 11.4.3 Engineering

Models of physical systems help engineers design and optimize structures, processes, and systems.

- **Example**: Finite element models of stress distribution in a bridge.

#### 11.4.4 Economics

Economic models help policymakers understand market dynamics and make informed decisions.

- **Example**: Supply and demand models to predict the impact of pricing changes on market equilibrium.

## Chapter 12: History of Mathematics

The history of mathematics is a fascinating journey through time, showcasing the development of mathematical concepts, techniques, and applications. This chapter explores the major milestones and influential figures that have shaped the field of mathematics.

### 12.1 Ancient Mathematics

#### 12.1.1 Mesopotamia and Egypt

- **Mesopotamia**: The Sumerians and Babylonians developed one of the earliest known number systems, which was sexagesimal (base-60). They made significant advancements in arithmetic, geometry, and algebra.
  - **Example**: The Babylonian tablet YBC 7289 demonstrates an approximation of the square root of 2.

- **Egypt**: The ancient Egyptians used a decimal (base-10) number system and made notable contributions to geometry, primarily for practical purposes such as land surveying and construction.
  - **Example**: The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus contains problems related to arithmetic, geometry, and algebra.

#### 12.1.2 Greece

- **Thales of Miletus**: Often considered the first mathematician, he introduced deductive reasoning in geometry.
- **Pythagoras**: Known for the Pythagorean theorem, which relates the sides of a right triangle.
- **Euclid**: Authored "The Elements," a comprehensive compilation of the knowledge of geometry at the time.
- **Archimedes**: Made significant contributions to geometry, calculus, and mechanics. Known for the Archimedean principle and approximations of pi.

#### 12.1.3 China and India

- **China**: The Chinese developed advanced techniques in arithmetic, algebra, and geometry. The "Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art" is a significant text that influenced East Asian mathematics.
- **India**: Indian mathematicians made remarkable contributions, including the development of the decimal number system and zero. Aryabhata and Brahmagupta made advancements in algebra, arithmetic, and astronomy.

### 12.2 Medieval and Renaissance Mathematics

#### 12.2.1 Islamic Golden Age

During the Islamic Golden Age, mathematicians made significant advancements and preserved Greek and Indian mathematical works.

- **Al-Khwarizmi**: Known as the "father of algebra," his works introduced systematic solutions of linear and quadratic equations.
- **Omar Khayyam**: Developed methods for solving cubic equations and contributed to the understanding of the binomial theorem.

#### 12.2.2 Europe

The Renaissance period saw the revival of interest in classical knowledge and significant advancements in mathematics.

- **Fibonacci**: Introduced the Fibonacci sequence and popularized the use of Hindu-Arabic numerals in Europe.
- **Nicolaus Copernicus**: Used mathematics to support his heliocentric model of the solar system.

### 12.3 Modern Mathematics

#### 12.3.1 17th Century

- **René Descartes**: Developed analytic geometry, which connects algebra and geometry.
- **Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz**: Independently developed calculus, providing powerful tools for understanding change and motion.

#### 12.3.2 18th Century

- **Leonhard Euler**: Made prolific contributions across various fields of mathematics, including graph theory and number theory.
- **Carl Friedrich Gauss**: Known as the "Prince of Mathematicians," he made significant contributions to number theory, statistics, and many other areas.

#### 12.3.3 19th Century

- **Niels Henrik Abel and Évariste Galois**: Developed group theory, laying the foundations for modern algebra.
- **Georg Cantor**: Founded set theory and introduced the concept of different sizes of infinity.
- **Sophie Germain**: Made substantial contributions to number theory and elasticity theory.

#### 12.3.4 20th Century

- **David Hilbert**: Formulated a list of 23 unsolved problems that guided much of the research in mathematics during the 20th century.
- **Alan Turing**: Laid the groundwork for computer science and artificial intelligence with his concept of the Turing machine.
- **John von Neumann**: Made contributions to quantum mechanics, game theory, and computer science.

### 12.4 The Impact of Mathematics on Society

#### 12.4.1 Science and Engineering

Mathematics is integral to scientific discovery and technological innovation, providing the language and tools needed to describe and understand natural phenomena.

#### 12.4.2 Economics and Finance

Mathematics underpins economic theories and financial models, helping to optimize resources, manage risks, and make informed decisions.

#### 12.4.3 Medicine and Biology

Mathematical models help to understand biological systems, design medical treatments, and analyze medical data.

#### 12.4.4 Everyday Life

Mathematics is essential in everyday activities such as budgeting, cooking, and problem-solving.

	 \[
	 \text{Bubble sort} \text{ compares each pair of adjacent items and swaps them if they are in the wrong order. This requires } n-1 \text{ comparisons for } n \text{ elements in the first pass, } n-2 \text{ comparisons in the second pass, and so on.}
	 \]

	 \]

   - **RSA Algorithm**:
	 \[
	 \text{RSA encryption involves two keys: a public key (e, n) and a private key (d, n). The public key is used to encrypt messages, and the private key is used to decrypt them. The keys are generated using two large prime numbers, } p \text{ and } q.
	 \]
	 \[
	 n = pq \text{ is the modulus for both keys, and } \phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1) \text{ is used to calculate the keys. The public key exponent } e \text{ is chosen such that } 1 < e < \phi(n) \text{ and } \text{gcd}(e, \phi(n)) = 1.
	 \]
	 \[
	 \text{The private key exponent } d \text{ is calculated as the modular multiplicative inverse of } e \mod \phi(n).
	 \]
